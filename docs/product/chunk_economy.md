# Chunk Inventory System
Chunks are collectible language units. Each chunk is a reusable piece of sentence power that the player earns, stores, and combines across scenarios.

The chunk inventory should function as the player's language backpack:
- It stores unlocked chunks in a visible, browsable Grammar Bank.
- It shows which chunks are already owned and which are still missing.
- It reminds the player that learned language remains usable beyond the current lesson.
- It turns sentence progress into an accumulating asset rather than a disposable exercise result.

Core rules:
- Chunks unlock through successful use, not passive exposure alone.
- Chunks should feel reusable and valuable after acquisition.
- Chunks should remain available for future recombination in new patterns and scenarios.
- The inventory should emphasize practical ownership: `I own this language piece now.`

Examples from the first slice:

| Chunk | Initial use | Future value |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `hungry` | `I am hungry.` | reusable in questions, feelings, and future daily-life scenarios |
| `seven years old` | `I am seven years old.` | reusable in self-introduction and identity scenes |
| `from Taiwan` | `I am from Taiwan.` | reusable in travel, school, and social introduction content |
| `my mother` | `This is my mother.` | reusable in family, home, and social presentation missions |
| `my brother` | `This is my brother.` | reusable in family, school, and story dialogue scenes |

The inventory should constantly reinforce that chunks are not answers the player used once. They are tools the player now carries.

# Chunk Rarity
Rarity should create excitement and memory value, but it should never block essential learning progression.

Suggested rarity tiers:
- Common
- Uncommon
- Rare
- Epic

Rarity should be based primarily on:
- flexibility across patterns and scenarios
- expressive power
- emotional memorability
- bundle size or phrase complexity

Design rules:
- Common chunks support core progression and appear early.
- Uncommon chunks add variation and identity.
- Rare chunks feel more expressive or more reusable across missions.
- Epic chunks should be emotionally memorable phrase rewards, not mandatory gatekeeping items.

Rarity is a motivational layer, not a scarcity wall. A learner should never fail to progress because a required chunk is too rare.

Suggested examples:

| Chunk | Suggested rarity | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `hungry` | Common | high-frequency beginner state with immediate reuse |
| `student` | Common | core identity chunk with broad early usefulness |
| `from Taiwan` | Uncommon | multi-word identity phrase with strong self-expression value |
| `good at swimming` | Rare | more expressive chunk with wider future synthesis potential |
| `my little brother` | Rare | emotionally rich family phrase with higher phrase complexity |

Early A1 content should use rarity mainly to create delight and memory, not to produce fear of missing out.

# Unlock Economy
Chunks should unlock through successful gameplay at moments that feel earned and emotionally timed.

Core principles:
- Unlocks occur after demonstrated correct use.
- Unlock timing matters as much as the chunk itself.
- Reward presentation should include visual and sound confirmation.
- Unlocks should reinforce replay by making successful repetition feel productive.
- Stronger unlocks should connect to mastery rather than raw attempt count.

Important balance rule:
- Not every correct answer should unlock something.
- Early game should unlock frequently to establish ownership and momentum.
- Later game should shift toward mastery, recombination, and set completion.

Unlock types:

| Unlock type | Definition | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First-time unlock | The player uses a chunk correctly for the first time | first correct use of `hungry` |
| Reinforcement unlock | Repeated correct use strengthens familiarity or adds inventory value | repeated clean uses of `from Taiwan` fill a familiarity meter |
| Mastery unlock | A chunk or pattern reaches a stable threshold and gains a marked mastery state | `I am {state}.` becomes mastery-linked after sustained accuracy |
| Combo unlock | A chunk or bonus reward is triggered by streak-based performance | a `Chunk Fusion` bonus after several fast correct answers |

Suggested early-game unlock flow:
1. First correct sentence produces a small unlock moment.
2. Repeated clean use fills visible progress toward mastery.
3. Strong performance in Drill or Rapid Response produces bonus recognition.
4. Scenario completion awards a set-based or synthesis-based unlock preview.

Emotional design target:
- First unlocks should feel frequent enough to hook the player.
- Later unlocks should feel earned enough to stay meaningful.
- The player should feel that improvement changes their inventory, not just their score.

# Sentence Forging
Sentence Forging is the act of combining chunks into complete sentences. It should feel closer to crafting than to worksheet completion.

Core metaphor:
- Patterns act like crafting templates.
- Chunks act like ingredients.
- Correct sentences feel forged, not merely selected.

Base example:

Template:
`I am {state}.`

Possible forged sentences:
- `I am hungry.`
- `I am tired.`
- `I am happy.`

Expanded examples:
- `I am from Taiwan.`
- `This is my brother.`

Design value:
- Forging promotes transfer learning because one stable frame supports multiple valid outputs.
- Forging creates ownership because the player feels they assembled the final sentence.
- Forging encourages experimentation because players want to test what other chunks can fit the template.

Crafting logic for the first slice:
- In `1-1 Personal Info`, the player forges identity and self-description lines.
- In `1-2 Family & Friends`, the player forges relationship lines using family chunks.
- Later scenarios should reuse these same chunks inside new templates to deepen retention.

The more the player feels they are building sentences out of owned parts, the less the system feels like grammar study.

# Cross-Scenario Reuse
Chunks should persist across worlds and scenarios. Reuse is essential for both product retention and learning retention.

Core rules:
- Once acquired, a chunk remains part of the player's long-term inventory.
- Old chunks should reappear in new contexts rather than being confined to their origin lesson.
- Reuse should feel like growing utility, not recycled content.

Example:
`hungry` learned in early daily-life content can later appear in:
- Restaurant
- School
- Travel
- Camping

Other examples:
- `from Taiwan` can return in social introductions, travel missions, and culture-themed scenes.
- `my brother` can return in school dialogue, home quests, and narrative NPC exchanges.

Why reuse matters:
- Repetition through reuse is better than isolated review because it ties memory to fresh meaning.
- Old chunks gain new emotional weight when they help solve new missions.
- Reuse makes the player feel stronger over time because earlier unlocks remain relevant.

The product should consistently communicate:
`You are not starting over. You are bringing your language kit into a new world.`

# Combo Bonuses
Combo bonuses should reward rhythm, automaticity, and clean execution rather than random grind.

Suggested bonus categories:
- combo streaks
- rapid-response streaks
- clean-run bonuses
- multi-pattern bonuses
- synthesis bonuses

Definitions:

| Bonus type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Combo streak | Consecutive correct sentence productions |
| Rapid-response streak | Consecutive correct answers within the target speed band |
| Clean-run bonus | Finishing a sequence without errors |
| Multi-pattern bonus | Successfully switching between more than one mastered pattern |
| Synthesis bonus | Combining chunks from different pattern families or scenarios |

Why combo matters:
- Combo systems reward automaticity directly.
- Combo systems create rhythm and flow during repetition.
- Combo rewards increase emotional momentum, making another round feel attractive.

Example feedback:
- `Combo x5`
- `Sentence Chain`
- `Perfect Response`
- `Pattern Mastery`
- `Chunk Fusion`

Example emotional progression:
- A beginner is happy to get one correct answer.
- A returning player wants to preserve a streak.
- A confident player wants to chain multiple patterns without breaking flow.

Combo bonuses should feel like proof that language production is becoming faster and more fluid.

# Collection Psychology
Collection psychology is the core retention engine of the chunk economy.

Humans naturally enjoy collecting reusable assets. In this system, chunk collection transforms grammar from an abstract subject into visible progression.

Core motivational effects:
- Ownership increases motivation.
- Visible completion encourages replay.
- Partial completion creates curiosity.
- Set-building makes repetition feel purposeful.

The system should include:
- collection pages
- missing chunk silhouettes
- chunk set completion
- scenario collection percentage
- family sets
- emotion sets
- travel sets

Examples of set logic:
- Emotion set: `hungry`, `tired`, `happy`, `fine`
- Family set: `my mother`, `my father`, `my brother`, `my sister`
- Identity set: `student`, `from Taiwan`, `seven years old`

Desired player feelings:
- `I want to complete this set.`
- `I unlocked a new sentence power.`
- `I can build more sentences now.`

Retention parallels:
- Pokemon-like collection psychology: players want to discover, own, and complete a roster.
- Card-game deck-building: players enjoy combining owned pieces into stronger or more flexible constructions.
- Minecraft-style recombination mindset: the joy comes from building new things out of reusable parts.

Why this matters for language learning:
- Grammar becomes progression instead of obligation.
- Missing pieces create soft curiosity rather than test anxiety.
- Completion tracking makes review feel like collection progress, not remediation.

The collection layer should never overshadow learning quality, but it should consistently make the learner want one more unlock.

# Long-Term Retention Design
Chunks should remain useful permanently. The long-term economy works only if old language pieces continue to matter.

Core rules:
- Old chunks should evolve into advanced combinations.
- Higher-level scenarios should reactivate beginner chunks.
- Chunk familiarity should reduce cognitive load over time.
- Reuse should help players spend attention on new structure rather than re-learning old meaning.

Examples:
- `hungry` begins as a simple self-state chunk, then returns in questions, dialogue, and scenario choices.
- `my brother` begins as a family label, then later supports introductions, comparisons, and story quests.
- `from Taiwan` begins as identity, then later supports travel and social conversation scenes.

Long-term learning goal:
- automatic sentence construction
- emotional attachment to language production
- voluntary repetition behavior

The ideal outcome is that the player no longer experiences old chunks as study content. They experience them as familiar tools that make new sentence construction easier and more satisfying.

# Design Constraints
- Avoid overwhelming inventory size early.
- Avoid meaningless unlock spam.
- Prioritize reusable chunks.
- Prioritize emotionally meaningful chunks.
- Avoid abstract grammar-first labeling.
- Every unlock should support future sentence generation.

The economy should reward the player for building a practical sentence toolkit, not for hoarding disconnected words.
